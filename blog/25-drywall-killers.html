The 25 Drywall Killers: Real Mistakes That Cost Real Money

These aren't hypotheticals. They're the deficiencies that show up on Tarion warranty claims, PDO punch lists, and final inspection reports — year after year, site after site. Learn them before they learn you.

The drywall phase looks simple from the outside. Boards go up, mud goes on, it gets painted. What could go wrong?

A lot. The drywall phase is where weeks of good rough-in work gets undermined in days. It's where pressure to meet closing dates compresses quality checks. It's where the most visible, permanent surface of every unit gets installed by crews working fast under tight margins.

These are the killers. The ones that generate callbacks, warranty claims, and the kind of purchaser complaints that follow a site super's reputation. Every one of these has been seen in the field — not once, but repeatedly.

The full list of 25 is a free download. What follows is the field commentary — the context that makes the list usable. Get both.

The Killers, With Context

  1. Butt joints on the same stud as seams When two board ends meet on the same framing member as an adjacent seam, you get stress concentration that shows up as cracking after the first heat cycle. Stagger them. Always.
  2. Missing vapour barrier seal at electrical boxes The vapour barrier is punctured for every single electrical box. Every one of those holes needs to be sealed with acoustical sealant before the board goes up. This is consistently missed on production sites and becomes a moisture complaint in year two.
  3. Board installed too tight to the floor Drywall on exterior walls should not contact the subfloor — moisture wicks. Standard practice is 12mm clearance minimum. Trim and baseboard covers it. What it won't cover is mould in the bottom 50mm of your perimeter walls.
  4. Fastener spacing violations OBC-referenced standards specify fastener spacing for ceilings and walls. Ceiling drywall particularly needs correct fastener schedules — too far apart and you get sag. This is one of the first things a Tarion inspector looks for.
  5. Ceilings boarded without checking deflection Drywall on ceilings with excessive joist deflection will crack at seams when the floor above is loaded. Check crown direction and live load deflection before boarding. Once it's on, you're dealing with it at the 30-day walkthrough.
  6. Nail pops from wet lumber Wood shrinks. When framing goes up wet and the building envelope closes fast, shrinkage happens after finishing. Screws are better than nails for this reason. If nails are used, oversizing the fastener doesn't help — the wood still moves.
  7. Horizontal board orientation on walls over 2.4m On 9-foot walls, horizontal installation creates a butt joint in the middle of the wall — the highest-visibility zone. Vertical installation with proper blocking eliminates mid-wall butt joints. Know when your spec requires which, and enforce it.
  8. No backing at tub deck perimeter The joint between drywall and tub or shower surround is a caulk joint, not a mud-and-tape joint. If there's no backing to properly terminate the board edge, you get a poorly supported caulk line that fails within the warranty period.
  9. Wrong board type in wet areas Regular drywall behind tile in shower surrounds is a Tarion claim waiting to happen. The spec calls for moisture-resistant board or cement board in wet areas. Substituting standard board because it's what's on the truck is not acceptable.
  10. Missing fire-rated board in required locations Attached garages, furnace rooms, and party walls in semi-detached and townhouse units have specific fire-separation requirements under OBC. Installing standard board where Type X is required is a code violation and a Tarion issue simultaneously.
  11. Compound applied over unfastened board If the board isn't fully fastened before the taper starts, movement after the fact creates cracking that shows through the final coat. The board has to be right before the mud starts.
  12. Overworked compound Three coats is the standard — tape, first, finish. Taperers who try to accelerate by loading the first coat thick create shrinkage cracking through all subsequent coats. Let each coat do its job.
  13. Insufficient drying time between coats Compound must be fully dry before the next coat. On production residential schedules, heat is cranked to speed drying — but inconsistent heat creates differential drying. The edges dry first; the centre stays wet; the surface skins over. Cracking follows.
  14. Bead popping at outside corners Metal corner bead applied to green board expands and contracts differently than the substrate. In unheated units, this is especially common in the first winter. Paper bead or vinyl bead performs better in this environment. Know what your spec allows.
  15. Inconsistent texture When two crews work different rooms on the same unit, texture variation becomes visible at paint. Stipple pattern, orange peel, and smooth all show differently under raking light. One crew per unit, or match the nozzle, pressure, and technique precisely.
  16. Ceiling drywall sagging between fasteners This is a fastener spacing issue that shows up immediately under raking light and gets worse over time. It's also visible from the stairs — the worst possible angle for a purchaser's first impression.
  17. Skipping priming before texture Texture applied to unprimed compound soaks in unevenly and flashes differently when painted. The result is a blotchy appearance that gets worse with every repaint. Prime the compound before texture — no exceptions.
  18. Poor board at stair stringers The underside of stairs is a high-visibility area with an awkward substrate. Inconsistent gaps, poor cutting, and inadequate backing at stringer intersections are visible every single time someone uses the stairs. Get the cut right.
  19. Missing blocking at wall-mounted hardware locations Towel bars, toilet paper holders, and handrails in bathrooms are warranty claims if they pull out of the wall. They pull out when they're fastened to drywall with no backing. The backing register has to be complete before boarding starts.
  20. Tape bubbling at seams Tape bubbles form when compound under the tape isn't fully bedded — air pockets trapped on application. Under raking light, these are immediately visible and they hollow out over time. Taper technique and supervision are the only solutions.
  21. Inadequate mud at inside corners Inside corners are finished in two steps — each wall face separately. When crews try to speed this by loading both faces simultaneously, you get mud piling up in the corner that shrinks, cracks, and becomes a recurring Tarion item.
  22. Drywall damaged by other trades after install Once the board is up, it's a target. HVAC, plumbers, electricians, and millwork crews all work in the same space. A site superintendent who doesn't establish and enforce protection protocols will spend weeks patching holes that were created by the trades who came after the drywall crew.
  23. No inspection before paint The window between final coat and paint is the last chance to catch everything while it's still repairable without painting over it. Walking every room under raking light before the painter starts is non-negotiable on a tight-tolerance site.
  24. Uneven fire-stop at penetrations Fire-rated assemblies are compromised by incomplete fire-stop at penetrations — plumbing stacks, electrical conduit, HVAC ducts. An inspector or home inspector will flag every one of these. Have a standard for what complete fire-stop looks like and walk it before closing.
  25. No site sign-off before board order The 25th killer is process, not product. Every rough-in should be signed off before drywall is ordered for the unit. When the board shows up before the electrician has finished, the pressure to install anyway is immense. Resist it. The sequence exists for a reason.
