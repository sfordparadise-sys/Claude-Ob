Tarion and the PDO: What Site Supervisors Actually Need to Know

The Pre-Delivery Orientation is the moment your warranty clock starts. Know what's required, what to document, what purchasers will flag, and how your sign-offs protect you — and your builder — down the road.

Most site supervisors think about Tarion in terms of what happens after possession — warranty claims, service requests, dispute resolution. But by the time a Tarion claim lands, the window to have prevented it has long passed.

The real leverage point is the PDO. The Pre-Delivery Orientation is the moment the unit transitions from construction to occupancy. How you run it, what you document, and what you sign determines how you'll be positioned for every warranty conversation that follows.

What Tarion Actually Requires

Under the Ontario New Home Warranties Plan Act (ONHWPA), builders registered with Tarion are required to provide warranty coverage on new homes. The warranty has three tiers:

  • 1-year warranty — Workmanship and materials defects, OBC violations
  • 2-year warranty — Electrical, plumbing, and heating delivery systems; building envelope water penetration; structural defects
  • 7-year warranty — Major structural defects

Each of these is triggered by possession. The PDO is what establishes the baseline — it documents the condition of the unit at the time of handover. What's noted at the PDO affects how subsequent warranty claims are evaluated.

The PDO: What Actually Happens

The PDO is a walkthrough of the completed unit with the purchaser before they take possession. The site supervisor — or their designate — walks every room with the purchaser, demonstrating systems and identifying the condition of the unit.

Items noted on the PDO form become builder-acknowledged deficiencies. Items that the purchaser claims post-possession but that were present at PDO become disputed — the builder's position will be that the purchaser accepted the unit in that condition.

This is why how you run the PDO matters. You need it to be thorough, documented, and signed.

What to Cover in the Walkthrough

  • All mechanical systems — HVAC operation, hot water, electrical panel, ventilation
  • All exterior features — grading, drainage, landscaping, driveway, exterior cladding
  • Garage — door operation, drains, fire-separation door hardware
  • All interior finishes — flooring, paint, millwork, tile, fixtures
  • Windows and exterior doors — operation, weather sealing, locks
  • All appliances (if supplied) — demonstrated and operational
  • Smoke and CO detectors — tested in the purchaser's presence
  • Any outstanding items from the construction completion checklist

Demo everything. Don't assume. If you don't demonstrate a system at the PDO and the purchaser claims it didn't work at possession, you have no documented position that it did. Operate every switch, every tap, every door.

The Warranty Timeline

Day 0
Possession / PDO

Unit is walked and signed off. The warranty clock starts. All noted deficiencies go on the PDO form and become acknowledged builder obligations.

30 Days
30-Day Form

Purchaser has 30 days to submit a form identifying additional deficiencies. Items on this form are evaluated against what was documented at the PDO.

Year 1
Year-End Form

A second submission window opens near the one-year anniversary. This is the last opportunity for workmanship and materials claims under the 1-year warranty.

Year 2
2-Year Warranty

Building envelope, mechanical systems, and structural claims. Envelope claims are the highest-frequency — moisture penetration, window leaks, and HVAC failures dominate this window.

Year 7
Major Structural

Covers defects in load-bearing elements that result in failure of the structural load functions of the home. The lowest-frequency, highest-consequence category.

What Site Supervisors Get Wrong

Rushing the PDO

A PDO should take 60 to 90 minutes for a typical low-rise residential unit. If it's taking 20, you're not doing it properly. Purchasers feel rushed and come back to the 30-day form with a list. Take the time at the PDO; you'll spend less time on claims later.

Not Documenting Cosmetic Acceptance

If a purchaser accepts a cosmetic item at the PDO — a minor paint touch-up, a scratch on a baseboard, a small grout repair — it needs to be on the PDO form as "accepted" or "noted for completion." An undocumented acceptance becomes a disputed claim.

Unsigned Forms

Both parties need to sign the PDO form. An unsigned form is no form. If a purchaser refuses to sign, document it — write the date, the time, and that they declined to sign, and have your representative countersign the notation. Do not leave a PDO without a signed document or a clear refusal notation.

Failing to Report Latent Defects

If you know there's an issue with a unit going into the PDO — a trade repair that's pending, a delivery outstanding, a system that wasn't fully commissioned — it needs to be on the list. Builders that hide known deficiencies create Tarion conciliation cases and enforcement exposure. Put it on the form with a completion commitment date.

The Site Super's Role in Purchaser Reporting

The PDO is not just about the unit condition. It's about the relationship. A site super who walks the PDO professionally — prepared, knowledgeable, unhurried, honest about what's outstanding — sets the tone for every subsequent interaction. Purchasers who feel respected in the PDO are far less likely to become adversarial warranty claimants.

Know the product. Know the unit. Know what's on the list. That's the job.
